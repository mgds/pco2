Our atmosphere is composed roughly of 21% oxygen (O2), 78% nitrogen (N2), 1% argon (Ar) and a range of trace gases such as CO2. The relative contributions of each gas to the atmosphere can be given as 1) their partial pressure (e.g., pCO2 is the partial pressure of CO2 in the atmosphere and is typically reported in units of microatmospheres, μatm) or 2) their concentration (e.g., xCO2 or mole fraction CO2 represents the relative quantity of CO2 molecules compared to all other gas molecules, except H2O, in the atmosphere). xCO2 is typically reported in units of parts per million (ppm) or parts per million by volume (ppmv), which are nearly identical numerically. xCO2 is independent of altitude, but pCO2 decreases with altitude, such that numerical values of pCO2 and xCO2 are nearly identical at sea level (where atmospheric pressure is ~1 atm), but differ at higher altitudes.
