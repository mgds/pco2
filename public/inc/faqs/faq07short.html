The known amount of CO2 emitted by fossil fuel burning, concrete production and land use changes (e.g., deforestation) is greater than the rise of CO2 in the atmosphere. This means that natural processes in the ocean and on land absorb a large portion of the emitted CO2, rather than contribute to it. In addition, two lines of evidence point to a predominantly (fossil) organic source of the carbon: 1) the observed decrease in atmospheric O2 indicates that reduced carbon (organic material such as coal, oil and gas) is being oxidized (i.e. burned or respired) and 2) the decrease in the stable carbon isotopic composition of atmospheric CO2 (13C/12C ratio) tells us that emitted CO2 is sourced from organic carbon (which is enriched in 12C). Volcanic eruptions can release CO2 quickly but the vast amount of emitted CO2 would require massive volcanism on a scale that cannot have gone unnoticed, and the carbon isotopic composition of volcanic CO2 would not decrease the 13C/12C ratio of atmospheric CO2 as observed.
