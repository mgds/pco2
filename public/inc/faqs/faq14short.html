Paleo-CO2 can be reconstructed from both terrestrial and marine archives. Each proxy comes with its own set of strengths and weaknesses, but what is common to all of them is that they lose sensitivity as atmospheric CO2 increases, meaning they provide more precise records at low than at high CO2 concentrations. Reconstructions from terrestrial archives are based on (1) the cellular structure and carbon isotopic composition (δ13C) of fossil leaves, (2) the δ13C values of calcium carbonate minerals formed in fossil soils, and (3) the presence and absence of nahcolite (i.e. sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3) and trona (i.e. sodium carbonate, Na3(HCO3)(CO3)Â·2H2O), two minerals that precipitate in evaporative but non-marine environments under specific CO2 concentrations. From the ocean, paleo-CO2 can also be reconstructed from (4) the δ13C of algae biomolecules such as alkenones, and (5) from the boron isotopic composition (δ11B) of planktic foraminifera shells.
