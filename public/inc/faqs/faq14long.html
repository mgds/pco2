Paleo-CO2 can be reconstructed from both terrestrial and marine archives. Each proxy comes with its own set of strengths and weaknesses, but what is common to all of them is that they lose sensitivity as atmospheric CO2 increases, meaning they provide more precise records at low than at high CO2 concentrations. Reconstructions from terrestrial archives are based on (1) the cellular structure and carbon isotopic composition (δ13C) of fossil leaves, (2) the δ13C values of calcium carbonate minerals formed in fossil soils, and (3) the presence and absence of nahcolite (i.e. sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3) and trona (i.e. sodium carbonate, Na3(HCO3)(CO3)·2H2O), two minerals that precipitate in evaporative but non-marine environments under specific CO2 concentrations. From the ocean, paleo-CO2 can also be reconstructed from (4) the δ13C of algae biomolecules such as alkenones, and (5) from the boron isotopic composition (δ11B) of planktic foraminifera shells.

Marine paleo-CO2 proxies are based on the fact that the ocean absorbs and releases CO2 according to its partial pressure in the atmosphere, and some marine plankton store information on the chemical composition of seawater in their molecular and skeletal structures. When plankton organisms die, their dead cells and skeletal structures settle on the seafloor and contribute to ocean sediments. As more and more sediment settles on top, the chemical and isotopic information that the plankton once incorporated in the surface ocean becomes archived in seafloor sediments. By taking sediment cores from the bottom of the ocean, paleoceanographers obtain a time line, with youngest sediments at the sediment-water interface and older sediments deeper and deeper below the seafloor. Paleoceanographers take discrete samples from these timelines and analyze their elemental and isotopic composition to reconstruct the plankton’s ancient growth conditions.

While reconstructions based on marine records translate surface ocean pCO2 to atmospheric pCO2 and are therefore presented in units of partial pressure (i.e. μatm), reconstructions from terrestrial archives are retrieved from environmental settings of variable altitude, and their CO2 estimates are presented as the mixing ratio or mole fraction, xCO2, which is reported in parts per million (ppm) or parts per million by volume (ppmv). The differences between partial pressure and mole fraction, pCO2 and xCO2 are approximately equal at sea level, and because xCO2 is constant with altitude, paleo-CO2 reconstructions from terrestrial and marine archives are numerically directly comparable and globally representative within their uncertainty bounds.

Several proxies use the carbon isotopic composition of fossil leaves, minerals or biomolecules in the process of estimating paleo-CO2. These approaches require knowledge of the δ13C value of CO2 in the paleoatmosphere and the most common way to estimate that for the past is by measuring the δ13C of foraminifera (a group of unicellular marine calcifiers), which is closely related to paleoatmospheric CO2 because CO2 is well mixed in the atmosphere and readily exchanges between the atmosphere and ocean (for more detail, see Tipple et al., 2010).

While this approach is generally agreed to be the best approximation at this time, there are several caveats this approach needs to consider, including the preferential (and well known) concentration (i.e. fractionation) of the heavier carbon isotope 13C into seawater compared to the atmosphere, the geographic variability of seawater-δ13C across different ocean regions (which can be resolved by focusing on subtropical open ocean sampling), and age differences between samples used for reconstructing atmospheric CO2 and δ13C. Reconstructions based on δ13C in fossil marine biomolecules can fortunately measure δ13C in foraminiferal shells from the same sediment samples, and thereby ensure that there is no difference in age between the shell and biomolecule measurements. The situation is more difficult for the terrestrial CO2 proxies, where expert judgment is required to ensure that the marine carbonate is the same age as the terrestrial sample. Fortunately, paleo-estimates of δ13Catm suggest that for most of the Cenozoic, changes in δ13Catm were relatively slow, meaning the error in CO2 estimates associated with aligning chronologies is probably not large.
