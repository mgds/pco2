In most plants, leaves are directly surrounded by atmosphere. Leaves are also the prime site for carbon assimilation (photosynthesis). For these reasons, some attributes of leaves are highly sensitive to the amount of CO2 in the atmosphere. Starting in the 1980’s, scientists observed that the density of stomatal pores often scales inversely to the growth concentration of atmospheric CO2. These stomatal complexes, which consist of the pore and two bounding guard cells, are the main sites for gas exchange, taking in CO2 and releasing O2 and water vapor (see figure). Because both water and CO2 are essential for plant life, there is strong evolutionary pressure for leaves to maximize the tradeoffs between CO2 uptake and water loss. This is why stomatal density is typically low when atmospheric CO2 is high: gathering CO2 is “easy” when it is plentiful, few stomata are required under such conditions, and a low stomatal density minimizes water loss. When CO2 is low, a high stomatal density is necessary to ensure sufficient carbon supply, albeit with a higher water cost.

The three bun-shaped cell structures are stomatal complexes, each with an elongate stomatal pore in the center that is surrounded by two kidney-bean-shaped guard cells. The other cells filling the rest of the space are epidermal cells. Scale bar = 25 μm. Photo courtesy of Jennifer Kowalczyk.

The response of stomatal density to CO2 can be measured in living plants, often using herbarium sheets with known leaf growth dates or experiments across a range of CO2. If leaf fossils contain cuticle (i.e. the waxy outermost layer of a leaf), stomata can be observed under a microscope because the cuticle contains an imprint of the outermost layer of cells (much like a wax seal). The calibrated responses from living species can then be coupled with measurements of stomatal density (# of stomata per mm2) in fossils to estimate paleo-CO2. A related metric called stomatal index (i.e. stomatal density / [stomatal density + epidermal cell density] × 100) is commonly measured instead because it is not affected by environmental factors that can change cell size, such as water stress (think of inflating a balloon with stomata and epidermal cells drawn on it; as the balloon expands, stomatal density will drop but stomatal index will remain fixed). There is also a third associated metric called the stomatal ratio, which relates the ratio of paleo-to-modern CO2 to the ratio of fossil-to-modern stomatal density (or stomatal index). The stomatal ratio assumes a prescribed response between CO2 and stomatal frequency (specifically, an inverse power law with the exponent fixed at -1), calibrated by one pair of CO2 and stomatal frequency measurements in the present-day (for more detailed information, please see Royer et al., 2019). The stomatal ratio is most often used when the nearest living relative of a fossil is not clearly known (this is most common in the pre-Cenozoic).

These three proxies based on stomatal frequency have been used in dozens of studies to produce hundreds of paleo-CO2 estimates. These methods are especially useful in the pre-Cenozoic, where very few other CO2 proxies can be applied. A general advantage of these proxies is that the material being measured (the leaves) was in direct contact with the paleoatmosphere. The proxies are also fairly straightforward to understand and implement (no expensive equipment is needed). However, these proxies do share some drawbacks. First, they are empirical. That is, a calibration is empirically measured in a living species (a one-point calibration in the case of the stomatal ratio) and then applied to fossils. The best proxies are ones that are based on a mechanistic understanding of the processes that control the relationship between CO2 and the proxy measurements. Second, the stomatal responses to CO2 are often species specific (this can even be true for different species in the same genus). This is a problem that generally affects all proxies that are produced by organisms. For the stomatal density and index proxies, this problem can be minimized by using only fossil species that are still alive today. In practice, this means that for the early Cenozoic only a handful of species are viable candidates (for example, Ginkgo). Third, calibration studies are time-consuming. Indeed, to date, less than 10 species of trees have been calibrated for the stomatal density and stomatal index proxies. This means that most of the paleobotanical record cannot be used. Finally, many species lose their sensitivity at high CO2 (above 500 to 1000 ppm), meaning that the upper uncertainties become unbounded.
