C3 land plant proxy (Leaf δ13C)
by Ying Cui, Matt Kohn, and Brian Schubert

Depending on their photosynthetic pathways, land plants can be categorized into C3 (trees, shrubs, herbs, and cool climate grasses such as wheat), C4 (warm climate grasses such as corn and sugar cane) and CAM (i.e. Crassulacean Acid Metabolism; succulents such as cactus and jade plant). About 85% of the land plant species on the Earth’s surface today are C3. In C3 plants, the initial photosynthetic carbon assimilation product is 3-phosphoglycerate (C3H7O7P, i.e., a molecule with 3 carbon atoms); C4 plants produce malic acid (C4H6O5, with 4 carbon atoms). C3 land plants have played an important role in the global carbon cycle since the rise of vascular plants (i.e. over the past ~400 million years).

All land plants preferentially take up 12C, leading to a relatively low 12C/13C ratio (i.e. low δ13C) in comparison with atmospheric CO2. Experimental studies (Schubert and Jahren, 2012) have shown that the difference between δ13C in the atmosphere and some C3 plants [i.e. δ13C = (δ13Catm - δ13Cleaf)/(1 + δ13Cleaf/1000)] depends quantitatively on the relative concentration of atmospheric CO2. The reason for this behavior has been hypothesized to reflect a CO2 sensitivity of enzymatic activity. Consequently, by relating δ13C in fossil leaf tissues to δ13C of CO2 in the atmosphere at the time the leaf grew (see General background and some commonalities of paleo-CO2 proxies), paleoclimatologists can reconstruct paleo-atmospheric CO2. Because other materials such as fossil bone collagen, tooth enamel and the calcium carbonate in stalagmites record the δ13C values of vegetation, scientists have also attempted to calculate δ13C from such records. Some of these studies find a close relationship with atmospheric CO2 (Breecker, 2017) whereas others find a closer correlation with the amount of mean annual precipitation (Kohn, 2016). Other environmental conditions such as plant functional type (i.e. gymnosperm vs. angiosperm) must be constrained as well, although in some settings these effects can be small. Similar to other proxies that are based on δ13C, the carbon isotopic composition of atmospheric CO2 can thereby be inferred from the δ13C value of benthic foraminifera (see General background and some commonalities of paleo-CO2 proxies).

δ13C as a proxy for paleo-CO2 has been tested against ice core data, and its precision has been assessed by Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation (Cui and Schubert, 2016). The C3 land plant carbon isotope proxy has the potential for wide applicability in the fossil record through the analysis of terrestrial organic matter, an abundant and commonly measured substrate that integrates carbon contributions across a large number of plants. However, although the prospect of high-resolution sampling is exciting, the use of bulk organic matter also introduces complications because different archives of plant carbon (e.g., lake sediments, soils, loess, etc.) record different absolute δ13C values, depending on the respective processes that transform raw leaves into the organic carbon that is measured. To circumvent this complication, most applications focus on Δ13C changes relative to a reference, such as a known Δ13C value at a known paleo-CO2 (e.g., from ice core measurements), or an independent estimate of pre-event or background paleo-CO2 (from other proxies), relative to which the CO2-change across the time period of interest can then be quantified (i.e., ΔCO2 =CO2(t) – CO2(t=0)).

Other challenges in applying this proxy include: 1) few plants have been studied experimentally, and different plant groups might show different responses of δ13C to paleo-CO2; 2) atmospheric oxygen (i.e. O2) concentrations also affect the carbon-isotope selectivity of the carbon-fixing enzyme Rubisco and cause greater fractionation (i.e. lower δ13Cleaf) at higher O2, but this effect remains poorly constrained; 3) although the proxy is highly sensitive at low CO2 (≤300 ppm), its uncertainty increases at higher atmospheric CO2. Similar to other CO2 proxies, the sensitivity of δ13C to CO2 is small at ~1000-2000 ppm. Finally, Δ13C also depends on other environmental variables such as water availability and plant community shifts, but very wet environments exert rather little influence over δ13C, and, in favorable cases, plant communities can be constrained. These corrections and uncertainties of the proxy systematics are reported in the form of propagated error bars of the resulting paleo-CO2 estimates.
