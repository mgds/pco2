  2. Paleo-CO2 Proxies

Earth’s geologic history has experienced a range of natural climate shifts that provide scientists with valuable information on the overall functioning and sensitivity of our climate system to natural cycles and perturbations, but geologic climate variables are rarely recorded directly. Antarctic ice core records form a rare exception, as enclosed air bubbles allow the direct measurement of Earth’s ancient atmosphere going back hundreds of thousands of years. However, current atmospheric CO2 exceeds the maximum values recorded in ice cores by more than 30%. To provide context for the future, we must look even further into the past, to times in Earth’s history when atmospheric CO2 was even higher than it is today. To do so, paleoclimatologists use a variety of ‘proxies’, i.e. biological and geochemical signatures of past atmospheric CO2 that can be elicited from fossils and minerals in terrestrial and marine archives.
